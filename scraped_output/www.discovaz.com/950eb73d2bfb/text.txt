Industry standard tools for jersey design

When you are ready to get serious, Adobe Illustrator is the gold standard for most pro designers. It allows you to work with complex layers and ensures that your logos and numbers are crisp. Another powerful choice is CorelDRAW Graphics Suite, which is also widely used in the apparel industry for its excellent layout capabilities. These programs allow you to manage technical details like bleed lines, which extend your artwork beyond the trim lines so that the color reaches every single edge of the jersey.

Choosing between these options depends on your workflow, but both will give you the control needed for professional results. Unlike basic online builders, these suites handle the specific color modes and high resolution requirements, usually between 150 to 300 DPI, that factories demand. By starting your project in a true vector environment, you save yourself the headache of redesigning everything later.

Bridging the Gap From AI Mockups to Vector Files

Imagine you just used an AI tool like the OnReplay Kit Generator to create a stunning jersey mockup in under 60 seconds. It looks incredible on your screen, but there is a catch. AI mockups are visualization files, not production-ready blueprints. To move from a cool picture to a real jersey, you need to recreate that design using professional builder software like Adobe Illustrator or CorelDRAW.

The secret sauce in this transition is vector file preparation for apparel. Unlike standard images that get blurry when you zoom in, vector files use mathematical paths to keep every line and logo perfectly sharp. This is essential for the factory to print your design across different sizes without losing any detail.

Understanding sublimation specs

When you move into the production phase, you have to play by the rules of sublimation printing. This process dyes the fabric directly, which allows for vibrant, all-over designs. However, it requires specific technical setups that differ from standard paper printing. For example, you must include bleed lines, which involve extending your artwork slightly beyond the actual trim lines. This ensures that when the fabric is cut and sewn, there are no white gaps at the seams.

One of the most surprising parts of this process is the color setup. While most printers use CMYK, modern sublimation workflows for 2026 often require the RGB color mode to achieve the brightest neon and deep contrast colors. You also need to ensure your file has a resolution of 150-300 DPI to maintain clarity during the heat transfer process.

High-resolution output between 150 and 300 DPI is a non-negotiable requirement for professional sublimation printing quality to ensure every detail remains crisp on the fabric.

Professional jersey software also helps you handle seam allowances and size grading. This means your design will automatically adjust so that a logo on a small jersey looks just as balanced as it does on an extra-large. Once your vector file is polished and matches these factory specs, you are ready to send it off. From there, the manufacturer takes over the physical production, turning your digital blueprint into a high-performance uniform.
